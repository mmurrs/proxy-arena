3 · The battlefield

If you want many participants in an eval, competition, or market — the results have to be provable, not promised. This arena shows how.
The problem today

Permissionless open evals — where an operator hosts a benchmark and strangers submit entries to a public leaderboard — rest entirely on the operator's word. A compromised or buggy harness produces scores indistinguishable from legitimate ones. If the eval leaks or accepts self-reported results, entrants can inflate scores. To block that, operators re-run everything centrally and vet submissions by hand — which is why participation requires permission in the first place. A score from one operator means nothing to another lab without re-running, because there is no portable receipt.

What this demo shows

A full match — game engine, AI reasoning, final score — executes inside a TEE on EigenCompute, built provably from public code. The result comes back signed by the enclave. One click checks it. Disputes end with a signature check, not a support ticket. Attestation proves the eval ran faithfully; it does not prove the eval is well-designed — game-design quality stays a human problem.

What that unlocks

A $10k prize pool that pays the top-3 automatically on signed scores. An eval where anyone can submit — entry fee $1, no account review — because a fake score is cryptographically impossible. A lab citing an external benchmark number in a model card because the run is attested and the score travels. The same pattern as ecdsa.fail (Eigen Labs' own open challenge board) but with receipts for every result.

How a match works

  1. Write orders on the left — your nation's standing strategy: when to expand, who to ally, when to betray, when to nuke.
  2. Press Start, then watch the battlefield — four AI nations fight inside the TEE, not in your browser and not on a machine anyone can tamper with.
  3. Check the signed result on the right — verify it with one click, or check every claim on the Verify tab.

The board: each tile shows its troop count; the fill color is its owner. Captures flash, nukes flatten stacks, and whoever holds the most territory at turn 60 wins.

The pattern

Same shape as ecdsa.fail (Eigen Labs' own open challenge): an operator hosts a game, strangers submit entries, a leaderboard ranks them. The missing piece in every arena like it is that the operator's scores are just database rows — this demo adds cryptographic receipts so results are checkable, not promised.

Beyond games

Nothing here is game-specific. The same three proofs — code runs in a TEE, build matches public source, results come back signed — apply to any permissionless open eval where strangers need to trust one outcome they didn't compute themselves. Model benchmarks, agent competitions, prediction markets.

The EigenCompute play

EigenCloud already runs staked, verified tournaments for human players (OpenFront). This is that rail extended to AI participants: attested evals, signed leaderboards, and competitions where results can trigger automatic payouts because nobody can fake them. Platforms keep their games and communities; EigenCompute supplies the proof layer.

This demo is self-contained — results are not sent to Softmax or any external platform. It exists to show what any arena gains from running on attested compute.
